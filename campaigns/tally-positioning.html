Tally has the best form builder on the market — and the most generic positioning

Tally · Series A July 15, 2026 Spent: $84 Validated

Why This Problem Caught My Eye

I found Tally through a Product Hunt thread where someone called it "the Notion of forms." That description stuck with me — because Tally's own website doesn't say anything close to that.

Their homepage opens with "The simplest way to create forms." That's the same claim Typeform, Google Forms, Jotform, and a dozen other tools make. It's accurate — Tally is simple — but it's invisible. In a category where everyone says "simple and easy," saying "simple and easy" is saying nothing.

Meanwhile, the thing that actually makes Tally different is buried below the fold: it works like a document. You don't drag and drop fields into a canvas. You just start typing, like you would in Notion or Google Docs, and a form materializes around your content. That's not a feature improvement — it's a fundamentally different paradigm for building forms. And they're not leading with it.

They have a Porsche and they're advertising it as "a car that drives."

Why I Wanted to Work on This

I've been tracking a pattern in PLG tools that raise Series A rounds: they retreat to safe, generic positioning right when they should be sharpening their wedge. The seed-stage pitch was specific and weird ("forms that work like docs!"). The Series A pitch gets boardroom-ified into something no one objects to and no one remembers ("the simplest way to create forms").

Tally is a clean test case for my emerging thesis: in crowded categories, shared-attribute positioning is invisible, and mechanism-based positioning outperforms it significantly. If I can prove this with $100 and a real ad test, that's a thesis entry with receipts.

The Diagnosis

I walked Tally's growth chain end to end:

  • Positioning (BROKEN): "The simplest way to create forms" is a shared attribute in a category where everyone claims simplicity. The homepage has no clear ICP — it speaks to "anyone who needs a form." The actual differentiator (document-style creation, unlimited forms for free) appears only after scrolling. A visitor from a Google Ad has about 5 seconds to decide if this is worth their time, and in those 5 seconds, Tally looks exactly like Typeform.
  • Acquisition: Solid SEO presence, growing Product Hunt mentions, active on Twitter. Not broken, but constrained by the positioning — the messaging that appears in search results and ads doesn't give anyone a reason to pick Tally over Typeform.
  • Activation: Actually great. The product itself delivers on the "just start typing" promise within seconds. The aha-moment is fast. Not the bottleneck.
  • Monetization: Freemium with generous free tier. Smart for PLG. Not the bottleneck.
  • Retention: Forms are project-based, so retention is naturally event-driven. No obvious leak.

The earliest broken link is positioning. Everything downstream (activation, monetization, retention) is working — but the front door doesn't tell visitors why they should walk through it instead of walking through Typeform's door. For a Series A company that needs to scale acquisition, this is the bottleneck: you can't pour more traffic into a page that doesn't convert because it doesn't differentiate.

The Hypothesis

"If I rewrite Tally's hero messaging around their actual differentiator ('Forms that feel like docs — just start typing') and test both versions with $75 of Google Ads on 'form builder' keywords, the differentiated version will produce at least 1.5x higher CTR, because it gives searchers a reason to click that no other search result offers."

What I Built

Two landing page variants built in Carrd ($9/year):

  • Variant A (Control) — mirrored Tally's current messaging: "The simplest way to create forms. Create forms for all purposes in seconds. Without knowing how to code."
  • Variant B (Differentiated) — led with the mechanism: "Stop dragging and dropping. Just type. Create forms that work like documents — no builder, no canvas, no learning curve. Just start writing and a form appears."

Both pages had the same layout, same CTA ("Try it free"), same color scheme adapted from Tally's brand. The only difference was the messaging.

Ad setup:

  • Platform: Google Ads
  • Keywords: "online form builder," "typeform alternative," "free form maker"
  • Budget: $75, split evenly between variants ($37.50 each)
  • Duration: 6 days
  • Ad copy for A: "Create forms the simple way — free form builder"
  • Ad copy for B: "Forms that work like docs — just start typing. Free."
  • Tracking: Google Analytics on both Carrd pages

Total spend: $75 on ads + $9 Carrd subscription = $84

Results

After 6 days and ~1,600 total impressions:

Metric A (Current) B (Differentiated) Delta
CTR 2.3% 5.1% +122%
CPC $1.82 $0.94 -48%
Bounce rate 68% 56% -12pts
CTR on "typeform alternative" 2.8% 6.8% +143%

The differentiated version outperformed on every metric. The strongest signal: on the "typeform alternative" keyword — people actively looking for something different — Variant B hit 6.8% CTR. These searchers already know what Typeform is. They want to know how you're different, not that you're "simple."

Hypothesis: validated. The differentiated version didn't just hit the 1.5x target — it more than doubled CTR. And the CPC drop means the same budget buys nearly 2x more clicks. Messaging change, zero product changes, $84 total cost.

What This Taught Me

Thesis update: In crowded categories, positioning on a shared attribute ("simple," "easy," "fast") is invisible — every competitor says the same thing, so the claim carries zero information. Positioning on a unique mechanism ("works like a doc, not a builder") gives the visitor a mental model of how your product is different before they've even tried it.

The mechanism doesn't have to be a feature. It can be the experience of using the product. "Just start typing" isn't a feature list — it's an experience description that makes you feel the difference.

This is my first data point for this claim. Confidence: medium. I want to see if the same pattern holds in 2-3 more crowded categories before I call it high-confidence.

What I'd Do Next (If Hired)

The experiment proved the messaging works better. Here's the full playbook I'd execute if I were brought on to own Tally's positioning and top-of-funnel:

  • 30 days: Rewrite the homepage, landing pages, and ad copy around the document-style mechanism. A/B test at scale with their real traffic (not Carrd proxies). Update meta descriptions and search snippets. Rewrite the Product Hunt tagline and App Store descriptions. Expected impact: 50-100% improvement in homepage conversion rate.
  • 60 days: Build a "Typeform vs Tally" comparison page optimized for the high-performing "alternative" keywords. Create a 30-second demo video that shows the document-style experience (screen recording of someone just typing and a form appearing). Test this video as a social ad on LinkedIn and Twitter targeting startup founders.
  • 90 days: Expand the positioning into content — blog posts and social content around "why form builders are broken" and the document-first approach. Identify the ICP segment that responds strongest (my guess: startup ops people and non-technical founders who hate learning new tools) and double down on that channel. Measure: homepage conversion rate, signup-to-first-form rate, CAC trend.
